There's a line I keep coming back to, attributed to various sources but perhaps best expressed by Antoine de Saint-Exupéry: *"Perfection is achieved, not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away."*

I think about this constantly in interface design. The instinct when building a product is always additive — add a feature, add a button, add a tooltip. But the hardest and most valuable skill is knowing what to remove.

## The Trap of Feature Completeness

Most software has too many controls. Not because the people building it are bad at their jobs — it's usually because every control made sense to someone at some point. A stakeholder asked for it. A user requested it. A competitor had it.

The problem is that features compound. Each new control adds cognitive load that isn't just the load of that one control — it's the load of understanding how it interacts with every other control.

Good interface design isn't about having fewer features. It's about revealing features at the right moment, in the right context, with the right amount of prominence.

## Progressive Disclosure

The principle that saves me most often is progressive disclosure: show only what's needed for the current step, reveal more as the user demonstrates intent.

This is why the best apps feel simple when you first open them, but reveal surprising depth as you explore. The depth was always there — it was just waiting for you to need it.

## What I Actually Do

In practice, my process looks like this:

1. **Start with the rarest action first.** What does a user do once? Put it behind a settings menu. What do they do every session? That earns a prominent position.
2. **Challenge every element.** For every UI element: if I removed this, what breaks? If the answer is "nothing obvious," it probably shouldn't be there.
3. **Let content breathe.** Whitespace isn't emptiness — it's structure. It tells the eye where to look.

The result is never a minimal interface for minimalism's sake. It's an interface where everything present has earned its place.
