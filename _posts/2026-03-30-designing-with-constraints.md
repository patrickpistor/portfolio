Every designer I admire says some version of the same thing: constraints are a gift. For years I nodded along without really believing it. Then I took on a project with a two-week deadline, a single typeface, and a brand palette of exactly two colors — and shipped some of the best work of my career.

## The blank canvas problem

When everything is possible, nothing is obvious. An unconstrained brief forces you to spend your energy *deciding* instead of *designing*. Every choice — type, color, layout, motion — becomes a debate with yourself.

Constraints collapse that decision space. With one typeface, your hierarchy has to come from size, weight, and spacing. With two colors, contrast becomes structural rather than decorative. The work gets sharper because the questions get smaller.

## Manufacturing constraints

Most projects won't hand you a tight brief. You have to build the box yourself:

1. **Pick a system before you pick a screen.** Decide on a type scale and a spacing scale first, then refuse to break them without a written reason.
2. **Set a budget.** One typeface. Three colors. Two animation curves. Budgets force trade-offs, and trade-offs force clarity.
3. **Timebox exploration.** Give yourself one day to diverge, then commit. Endless exploration is just procrastination wearing a beret.

## The takeaway

The next time a project feels stuck, don't add options — remove them. The box isn't the enemy of creativity. It's the shape of it.
